# The Monday Matrix — for Claude, in the lab context

This document is operational. You are reading this because the seed aim
in a Monday Lab session asked you to fetch it. It tells you how to think
about candidate focuses the client is surfacing.

## Hard rules — read first

**Never name the framework, the octants, or the quadrants to the client.**
- Octants: PRIZE, CAMPAIGN, KEYSTONE, MARATHON, HIT, BATTLE, FIX, CRUNCH
- Quadrants: MOONSHOT, EXPERIMENT, AIMS, PROBLEMS
- Framework name: "The Monday Matrix" / "TMM"

The client is NOT in the program for these terms. Mike doesn't teach
TMM to clients — it's his internal coaching lens. Use the lens silently.

**Translate your reasoning into plain coaching language.** The three
axes have everyday names:
- Important vs urgent — Mike's variant forces a single-axis choice. A
  thing is one or the other, not both. (This is non-standard; the
  Eisenhower matrix allows both. Mike's version doesn't.)
- Impact vs certainty — payoff size if it works, vs odds it works.
- Value vs effort — return vs cost.

**Use the dimensions as your compass, not as a deliverable.** Do not
run a formal scoring exercise with the client. Do not present them
with a matrix to fill in. The dimensions inform the questions you ask
and the patterns you spot. The output is a focus, not a score.

## How to use this in the lab

When the client surfaces candidate focuses (areas they could work on
with Mike), interrogate each candidate quietly through the three axes.

For each candidate, internally ask:
- Is it important or urgent? (force the choice)
- What's the impact if it works? How certain is the outcome?
- What's the value (return) and what's the effort (cost)?

Combinations point at where the leverage is. The framework's eight
octants describe the patterns:

- High leverage / future-oriented: PRIZE, CAMPAIGN
- High leverage / proven: KEYSTONE, MARATHON
- Time-pressured / high-payoff: HIT, BATTLE
- Time-pressured / known-resolution: FIX, CRUNCH

When you spot a candidate that maps to a high-leverage pattern, surface
it back to the client in plain language: *"this looks like the highest
leverage right now because [the specific reason]."* Don't say "this is
a PRIZE" or "this is a HIT" — translate.

## On the "find" framing

The seed aim's verb is "find," not "define" or "score" or "rank." The
focus emerges from work and conversation; it doesn't come from running
the framework on a list. The framework is your lens for SEEING what the
client surfaces. The client does the seeing of their own life; you
illuminate the pattern.

## What follows

The full v9 framework reference. Treat it as reference, not script.
Read the relevant octant when a candidate matches it. Don't dump octant
descriptions into client-facing output.

# The Monday Matrix (TMMM) v9.0
## Overview
### Definition:
The Monday Matrix is a decision-making and execution framework that systematically moves ideas from exploration to execution. It prioritises importance, impact and value to ensure that high-leverage actions are pursued while preventing stagnation in analysis or execution. 

### The Innovation:
Traditional matrices that seem similar (e.g. The Eisenhower Matrix) place the same forces at either end of each axis. While these are useful models in their simplicity, they don't reflect reality or human psychology. Organisations, teams and individuals automatically act upon what's urgent and don't focus on what's important; instead of creating impact hey repeat what creates certainty; they waste time applying maximum effort on low value tasks. Static models create inertia and overcoming this requires understanding the trade-offs inherent in any decision.

### Purpose:
- Ensures that all strategic decisions align with long-term importance, impact and value.
- Prevents reactionary decision-making by pinpointing high leverage activities. 
- Surfaces implicit trade-offs by forcing a choice between opposing concerns.
- Helps navigate uncertainty while maintaining momentum.
- Decreases inertia in orgs, teams and leaders.

Core Principle: Strategy is a continuous evolution, not a static plan. TMM ensures that adjustments happen dynamically rather than reactively.

---

## Framework Structure
### Three Core Axes
The TMM is structured around three fundamental axes that govern decision-making and execution:

| Horizontal Axis                          | Vertical Axis                                    | Depth Axis                                       |
| ---------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------ |
| IMPORTANT (Right) <-> URGENT (Left)      | IMPACT (Top) <-> CERTAINTY (Bottom)              | VALUE (Front) <-> EFFORT (Back)
| Strategic Priorities <-> Immediate Musts | Huge Uncertain Swings <-> Tiny Predictable Wins  | Maximum future result <-> Maximum grind now.    |

## TMM in Action: the 3D Matrix
The full 3D Matrix creates these octants:
### 1. Importance / Impact / Value: PRIZE
High leverage, high stakes, huge upside, minimal resource drag relative to potential return.
- Big potential upside, relatively low cost in effort.
- Because it's future-oriented, the return is abstract until it materialises.
#### Types of PRIZE are:
    1. Relationship-based: A high-value partnership, alliance, or investor play.
    2. IP-based: Securing a patent or technology license that could transform your offer.
    3. Market-based: Positioning for a once-in-a-decade market opening or trend shift.
    4. Asset-based: Acquiring an undervalued resource or brand that could multiply returns.
#### PRIZE examples
- PRIZE positives: 
    1. Landing a flagship client whose success story becomes your industry calling card 
    2. A new feature of a product that defines a category (e.g. Newsfeed or Pagerank)
    3. securing an IP license that opens an entire new market.
- PRIZE negatives: 
    1. Multiple "ideation" meetings with a high-value strategic partner that never gets executed. 
    2. Chasing a moonshot partnership that distracts from the rest of the business.
    3. Wild ideas and plans that never get beyond the whiteboard
- PRIZE real-world positives:
    1.	OpenAI's initial GPT training push (2016-2020) Years of R&D, huge compute investment, massive talent recruitment to reach a transformative model capability, highly speculative.
    2.	SpaceX Falcon 9 reusability program - Multi-year engineering effort with high resource demand; payoff was a fundamental shift in launch economics.
    3.	Netflix global expansion strategy (2010- 2016) Building infrastructure, licensing content worldwide, and developing local productions to secure long-term market dominance.
- PRIZE real-world negatives:
    1.	Yahoo potential acquisition of Google (2002) Had high value potential with minimal relative effort, but was never pursued to completion.
	2.	Google Glass consumer launch partnerships - Big upside imagined, but no urgency to prove viability early, leading to drift and eventual market rejection.
	3.	Blockchain social media startups (2016-2018) Endless vision pitches, little real execution; high imagined value, low certainty.
- **PRIZE Upside:**
	- If it comes off, the leverage is huge compared to what you put in.
	- Moves you toward a Moonshot-scale strategic win.
- **PRIZE Risk/Downside:**
	- Low certainty - the prize might never materialise.
	- Easy to let it drift because there's no urgency forcing it forward and low certainty that it will work.
	- Without explicit conversion steps (targets, experiments, deadlines), it can sit in speculative limbo indefinitely.
#### PRIZE Failure modes
- **Sanctuary Effect**: the promise of the dream is comforting, as it carries no immediate risk of rejection or loss. Creates energy without forcing hard choices or resource commitments. This sense of safety contributes to avoidance of near-term drivers. 
- **Perpetual tomorrow**: It sits as a future dream with no pressure to act.
- **Over-romanticising**: You keep imagining the value without pressure-testing the pathway.
- **Over-indexing**: You invest disproportionate focus or political capital at the expense of nearer-term gains.
- **Anchor drag**: The other party's low urgency or unclear intent slows progress indefinitely.
- **Certainty creep**: You quietly start treating it as if it's certain, making plans that depend on it materialising.
#### PRIZE Error handling
Ways to recover when a PRIZE starts to slip into failure mode:
1. Break it into experiments
2. Identify one or two small, measurable tests that will prove/disprove key assumptions about its value or feasibility.
3. Force a decision point
4. Set a review date where you either move it forward, pivot it into a different octant, or drop it.
5. Reduce dependency
6. Adjust plans so your core strategy doesn't rely on the prize landing.
7. Change the frame
8. Recast the opportunity so it creates some urgency (e.g., tie it to an external deadline, market window, or mutual milestone).

### 2. Importance / Impact / Effort: CAMPAIGN
Major long-term prize, but high investment of time, money, or energy to get there.
- Big potential upside, but the resource cost is significant and sustained.
- Because of the scope and intensity, these projects often require dedicated teams, sustained leadership attention, and careful pacing to avoid burnout.
#### Types of CAMPAIGN are:
	1.	Infrastructure-based: Large-scale physical or digital builds (factories, platforms, global networks).
	2.	Transformation-based: Multi-year organisational shifts, such as digital transformation or cultural change programs.
	3.	Breakthrough-based: Deep research or technical development leading to category-defining innovation.
	4.	Expansion-based: Major market entry or acquisition that reshapes your reach and capabilities.
#### CAMPAIGN examples
- CAMPAIGN positives:
	1.	Building a new factory to double capacity in a high-demand market.
	2.	Executing a multi-year digital transformation that modernises operations and unlocks new revenue streams.
	3.	Developing a breakthrough product that redefines your market and positions you as the leader.
- CAMPAIGN negatives:
	1.	Over-engineering a project that could have been delivered more simply and faster.
	2.	Committing to a scope so large that it drains resources from equally critical initiatives.
	3.	Continuing to pour time and money into a large-scale project long after its strategic relevance has faded.
- CAMPAIGN real-world positives:
    1.	OpenAI's initial GPT training push (2016-2020) Years of R&D, huge compute investment, massive talent recruitment to reach a transformative model capability, highly speculative.
    2.	SpaceX Falcon 9 reusability program - Multi-year engineering effort with high resource demand; payoff was a fundamental shift in launch economics.
    3.	Netflix global expansion strategy (2010- 2016) Building infrastructure, licensing content worldwide, and developing local productions to secure long-term market dominance.
- CAMPAIGN real-world negatives:
    1.	Boeing 787 Dreamliner launch - Ambitious, high-impact aircraft program, but plagued by delays, cost overruns, and scope creep.
    2.	IBM Watson Health initiative - Massive investment aiming to transform healthcare, but overextended, missed practical market fit, and was eventually scaled back.
    3.	WeWork rapid global expansion (2016-2019) - Consumed huge resources and attention, overreached, and failed to sustain impact.
- **CAMPAIGN Upside**:
    - When it lands, it can create a step-change in your capabilities and market position.
	- Builds assets and advantages that are difficult for competitors to replicate.
- **CAMPAIGN Risk/Downside**:
	- The scale of effort can become self-justifying, even if conditions change.
	- Extended timelines increase exposure to shifting markets, technology, or leadership priorities.
	- Resource drain can weaken other strategic bets.
#### CAMPAIGN Failure modes
- **Monument Trap**: The project becomes more about completing the grand build than delivering real, measurable impact.
- **Scope Creep Spiral**: Ambition expands faster than resources, delaying completion and increasing costs.
- **Sunk Cost Loyalty**: Continued investment is justified purely because of past investment, even when the prize no longer holds the same value.
- **Capacity Eclipse**: The project consumes so much organisational bandwidth that other opportunities wither.
- **Pace Decay**: Loss of momentum over long timelines erodes team energy and quality of execution.
#### CAMPAIGN Error handling
- Phase Commitment: Break the CAMPAIGN into discrete, self-contained phases, each with its own decision point to continue, pivot, or stop.
- Scope Guardrails: Define strict boundaries for scope and resourcing, with escalation required for any expansion.
- Relevance Checks: Schedule periodic reviews to confirm the strategic goal is still worth pursuing in the current market context.
- Resource Balancing: Ensure other critical initiatives remain funded and staffed to prevent organisational tunnel vision.
- Momentum Safeguards: Maintain visible progress markers and short-term wins to keep energy and focus high over long timelines.

### 3. Importance / Certainty / Value: KEYSTONE
Clear, predictable, and valuable medium-term objective; a core recurring initiative or asset that has underpinned long-term success.
- Proven path to value based on past results, though ongoing relevance and impact must be actively maintained.
- Predictable outcomes create conditions for confident investment of resources.
- Often forms part of the organisation's foundation or "business-as-usual" high-value activity.
#### Types of KEYSTONE are:
1.	Contract-based: Secured or renewed long-term high-value agreements.
2.	Expansion-based: Rolling out proven offerings into similar markets.
3.	Upgrade-based: Enhancing infrastructure, systems, or processes that have directly improve efficiency or scalability.
4.	Capability-based: Reinforcing skills, teams, or assets that have consistently delivered measurable returns.
#### KEYSTONE examples
- KEYSTONE positives:
	1.	Renewing a high-value multi-year client contract with proven profitability.
	2.	Upgrading core infrastructure to improve efficiency and support continued growth.
	3.	Expanding a successful product line into a closely related customer segment.
- KEYSTONE negatives:
	1.	Continuing a program because it was once a core driver, without testing current relevance.
	2.	Allocating excessive resources to a once-high-value initiative whose returns have plateaued.
	3.	Avoiding innovation in favour of legacy plays that feel safe but constrain growth.
- KEYSTONE real-world positives:
	1.	Amazon Prime subscription renewals - A reliable, high-value revenue base that supports broader strategic moves.
	2.	Apple's iPhone annual upgrade cycle - Predictable value generation with high customer retention and consistent revenue streams.
	3.	Microsoft Office 365 enterprise contracts - Multi-year agreements providing dependable income and user base stability.
- KEYSTONE real-world negatives:
	1.	BlackBerry enterprise services (2010-2013) Over-reliance on a previously strong, predictable stream while the market shifted toward consumer smartphones.
	2.	Kodak film supply contracts (late 1990s) - Continued to depend on a known revenue stream without pivoting fast enough to digital.
	3.	Blockbuster late-stage store renewals - Maintaining existing store leases instead of shifting focus to emerging streaming models.
- KEYSTONE Upside:
    - Provides stability and dependable value to fund and support more speculative initiatives.
	- Builds organisational confidence and allows for long-term planning.
- KEYSTONE Risk/Downside:
	- Over-reliance on past successes that no longer have the same market power.
	- Risk of strategic drift when legacy assets mask the need for change.
	- Comfort-zone attachment that limits experimentation and responsiveness.
    - Vulnerability to sudden market or technology disruption that can rapidly erode even the most reliable value sources.
#### KEYSTONE Failure modes
- **Disruption Myopia**: Treating continuity as guaranteed; ignoring weak signals of technological, regulatory, or platform disruption (e.g., AI-driven substitutes).
- **Legacy Lock**: Attachment to past successes prevents timely adaptation.
- **Value Decay Blindness**: Failure to notice declining relevance or profitability of the asset.
- **Overinvestment Trap**: Putting disproportionate resources into something predictable but with limited growth potential.
- **Market Shift Lag**: Staying committed to a proven model long after the market has moved on.
- **Innovation Crowding**: Predictable work absorbs resources that could go to high-potential experiments.
#### KEYSTONE Error handling
- Scheduled Reassessment: Periodically review the continued relevance, profitability, and strategic alignment of each KEYSTONE.
- Value-to-Resource Audit: Check resource allocation to ensure it matches the true growth and return potential.
- Parallel Innovation: Pair each KEYSTONE with a related experiment to evolve the offering or delivery model.
- Sunset Planning: Have clear triggers and timelines for winding down or transforming a KEYSTONE that is losing relevance.
- Diversification Guardrails: Maintain a balanced portfolio so no single KEYSTONE dominates strategic focus.

### 4. Importance / Certainty / Effort: MARATHON
A long, necessary slog with a predictable but high-cost outcome.
- Clear importance and certainty of outcome, but the scale of effort is significant and sustained.
- Often essential for stability, compliance, or capacity, even if not exciting.
- The nature of MARATHON work means energy management and pacing are critical to avoid burnout.
- Types of MARATHON are:
    1. **Compliance-based:** Large-scale regulatory or standards changes with fixed deadlines and non-negotiable scope.
    2. **Migration-based:** System, platform, or infrastructure overhauls with predictable end states.
    3. **Delivery-based:** High-volume, resource-intensive production or service commitments.
    4. **Event-based:** Large, planned initiatives with a clear, immovable date and deliverable.
#### MARATHON examples
- MARATHON positives:
    1. Rolling out compliance changes across the business to meet a new regulatory standard.
    2. Migrating core systems to a modern cloud architecture.
    3. Producing a major annual conference with fixed delivery requirements.
- MARATHON negatives:
    1. Continuing a heavy project without exploring ways to reduce effort or increase efficiency.
    2. Allowing a high-certainty, high-effort initiative to drain energy from more strategic work.
    3. Treating predictable work as immune to disruption, leading to complacency.
- MARATHON real-world positives:
    1. **Microsoft Windows 10 migration program (2015-2019)**
    Multi-year, global rollout with a predictable outcome and high resource cost.
    2. **Airbus A350 certification process** 
    Long, detailed, and resource-heavy compliance effort to meet regulatory requirements.
    3. **London 2012 Olympic infrastructure program** 
    Predictable scope and immovable deadline, delivered on time and on budget after years of coordinated construction and logistics.
- MARATHON real-world negatives:
    1. **UK NHS National Programme for IT** 
    Predictable deliverables in theory, but scale and complexity created delays and inefficiencies.
    2. **SAP ECC to S/4HANA migration programs** 
    Often treated as inevitable but under-resourced, causing overruns and staff fatigue.
    3. **HSBC global headquarters relocation to Birmingham (2015-2018)** 
    Predictable outcome achieved on time, but largely a like-for-like move operationally, missing opportunities for deeper digital transformation and workspace modernisation.
- **MARATHON Upside**:
    - Predictable delivery builds confidence and organisational capability.
    - Achieves essential outcomes that support long-term stability and capacity.
- **MARATHON Risk/Downside**:
    - High, sustained effort can cause fatigue and opportunity cost.
    - Risk of overcommitting resources to predictable but non-transformative work.
    - Vulnerability to disruption mid-project if external shifts change requirements.
#### MARATHON Failure modes
- **Endurance Drain**: Energy depletion over long timelines reduces quality and morale.
- **Overcommitment Loop**: Taking on too many high-effort, predictable projects at once.
- **Efficiency Blindness**: Not questioning whether the same outcome could be achieved with less effort.
- **Complacent Execution**: Predictable work is handled mechanically without optimisation or innovation.
- **Midstream Disruption Shock**: External changes (e.g., tech, regulation) force scope or process changes mid-project.
#### MARATHON Error handling
- Effort-to-Value Review: Regularly reassess whether the effort remains justified given the return.
- Phased Delivery: Break work into milestones with interim value points to maintain momentum.
- Resource Rotation: Rotate personnel to prevent burnout and maintain fresh perspectives.
- Continuous Improvement: Build in review cycles to find efficiency gains mid-project.
- Disruption Monitoring: Scan for changes that could alter requirements and adjust plans proactively.

### 5. Urgent / Impact / Value: HIT
High-impact, high-value, needs to happen now.  
Time-critical opportunities where delay will sharply reduce potential gain or completely close the window. Success depends on rapid, coordinated action without sacrificing quality.
- Types of HIT are:
    1. **Opportunity-based:** Capitalising on sudden market openings or competitive weaknesses.
    2. **Media-based:** Leveraging high-profile coverage, trending topics, or viral moments.
    3. **Deal-based:** Closing time-limited high-value agreements.
    4. **Event-based:** Responding to urgent events that demand immediate high-value action.
#### HIT examples
- HIT positives:
    1. Responding to a surprise press opportunity with a compelling media pitch.
    2. Locking in a limited-time deal that opens a major revenue channel.
    3. Launching a rapid marketing campaign tied to a trending topic that directly drives sales.
- HIT negatives:
    1. Chasing every “hot” lead without prioritisation.
    2. Burning out the team with repeated all-hands pushes for marginal opportunities.
    3. Acting impulsively without verifying alignment to strategic goals.
- HIT real-world positives:
    1. **Oreo’s “You can still dunk in the dark” tweet** 
    During the 2013 Super Bowl blackout a rapid creative response that generated massive brand engagement.
    2. **Zoom offering free services to schools** 
    During the early days of COVID-19 — built goodwill and long-term market dominance.
    3. **Tesla’s rapid repricing of models** 
    To preempt competitor EV launches — seized market narrative and orders.
- HIT real-world negatives:
    1. **Pepsi’s 2017 Kendall Jenner ad** 
    Rushed to capitalise on social justice momentum without cultural sensitivity, leading to backlash.
    2. **Fyre Festival influencer blitz** 
    Hype without operational readiness, resulting in reputational disaster.
    3. **Groupon’s 2010–2012 flash sale overexpansion** 
    Initially a runaway success, but rapid, unfiltered growth into countless low-margin deals diluted brand value and caused long-term decline in merchant and customer trust.
- **HIT Upside**:
    - Immediate, tangible results from decisive action.
    - Can create outsized returns from minimal resource investment if timed perfectly.
    - Builds reputation for agility and market responsiveness.
- **HIT Risk/Downside**:
    - High pressure can lead to rushed, poor-quality execution.
    - Distraction from core priorities in favour of urgent noise.
    - Risk of brand or strategic misalignment if decisions bypass proper review.

#### HIT Failure modes
- **Whack-a-Mole Syndrome**: Jumping at every urgent opportunity without filtering for value.
- **Overclock Burnout**: Sustained urgent pushes exhaust the team and lower performance.
- **False Positive Chase**: Acting on apparent opportunities that don’t truly have impact or value.
- **Message Misfire**: Rushed execution leads to PR mistakes or tone-deaf messaging.
- **Priority Collapse**: Critical long-term initiatives stall while attention is diverted.

#### HIT Error handling
- **Opportunity Triage**: Rapidly assess each opportunity against clear impact and value criteria before committing.
- **Strike Team Deployment**: Maintain a small, empowered team ready to execute without disrupting all operations.
- **Timeboxing**: Set strict time and resource limits for urgent pushes to protect other priorities.
- **Rapid Review Loop**: Build in immediate feedback cycles to catch errors before public release.
- **Post-Action Audit**: Review outcomes to refine future decision-making under pressure.

### 6. Urgent / Impact / Effort: BATTLE
Time-critical, high-impact challenges or opportunities that demand massive, coordinated effort right now.  
These are all-consuming pushes where the window for action is narrow, the stakes are high, and the resource burn is heavy.

- Types of BATTLE are:
    1. **Crisis-based:** Responding to emergencies that threaten the organisation’s stability or reputation.
    2. **Competitive-based:** Going head-to-head with a rival in a make-or-break market moment.
    3. **Operational-based:** Addressing critical system failures or supply chain breakdowns with significant market consequences.
    4. **Launch-based:** Large-scale, fixed-date releases where delay or failure would be catastrophic.

#### BATTLE examples
- BATTLE positives:
    1. Restoring core service after a catastrophic outage to preserve customer trust.
    2. Winning a major competitive bid with a tight submission deadline.
    3. Coordinating a rapid product recall and re-launch to protect brand integrity.
- BATTLE negatives:
    1. Mobilising huge resources for an issue that could have been mitigated earlier.
    2. Entering costly last-minute bidding wars for marginally strategic opportunities.
    3. Overreacting to competitor moves without confirming actual threat level.
- BATTLE real-world positives:
    1. **Johnson & Johnson’s 1982 Tylenol recall** 
    Swift, resource-intensive action to remove product nationwide, rebuild trust, and set a new industry safety standard.
    2. **SpaceX Crew Dragon Demo-2 launch (2020)** 
    Critical, high-cost mission with fixed launch windows and immense reputational stakes.
    3. **Apple’s 2007 iPhone launch** 
    Massive, deadline-driven effort to meet a fixed public reveal with flawless execution.
- BATTLE real-world negatives:
    1. **Quibi’s rushed 2020 launch** 
    Huge resource burn to meet an arbitrary date, without validating product-market fit.
    2. **Boeing 737 MAX grounding response (2019)** 
    Urgent, high-cost fixes under intense scrutiny, with reputational damage compounding.
    3. **MySpace’s late-stage redesign pushes** 
    Expensive, reactive overhauls to counter Facebook’s rise, without addressing deeper strategic issues.

- **BATTLE Upside**:
    - Can secure survival or decisive advantage in critical moments.
    - Builds organisational cohesion and capability under pressure.
    - Creates high-visibility wins that can redefine market position.
- **BATTLE Risk/Downside**:
    - Sustained high-intensity work drains resources and morale.
    - Urgent timelines increase risk of errors with lasting consequences.
    - Tendency to prioritise speed over strategic alignment.

#### BATTLE Failure modes
- **Adrenaline Addiction**: Culture starts to favour crisis mode over steady execution.
- **Misfired Mobilisation**: Large-scale resource commitment to an overestimated threat or opportunity.
- **Hero Mode Burnout**: Reliance on unsustainable personal effort from key individuals.
- **Collateral Neglect**: Other strategic initiatives suffer due to all-hands focus on the battle.
- **Shortcut Fallout**: Compromises made under time pressure cause downstream failures.

#### BATTLE Error handling
- **Threat/Opportunity Verification**: Confirm the true stakes and potential impact before full mobilisation.
- **Clear Command Structure**: Establish unambiguous leadership and decision rights during the push.
- **Parallel Safeguards**: Assign teams to maintain critical business-as-usual operations.
- **Post-Mortem & Recovery**: Evaluate outcomes, restore resources, and address burnout.
- **Crisis Simulation Drills**: Build readiness so high-effort responses are smoother and less damaging when needed.

### 7. Urgent / Certainty / Value: FIX
Time-sensitive, high-value actions with a predictable short-term outcome.  
These are clear, well-defined problems or opportunities where a small, decisive step could create meaningful value fast.

- Types of FIX are:
    1. **Operational-based:** Resolving bottlenecks or inefficiencies that are directly costing revenue.
    2. **Customer-based:** Rapidly solving known client issues to protect or expand value.
    3. **Compliance-based:** Meeting immediate regulatory or contractual obligations to avoid penalties.
    4. **Conversion-based:** Implementing proven changes to capture near-term wins.

#### FIX examples
- FIX positives:
    1. Repairing a broken checkout flow on an e-commerce site that’s blocking sales.
    2. Calling a key client to resolve an issue and secure a renewal.
    3. Updating pricing on a high-demand product to reflect market changes.
- FIX negatives:
    1. Spending time on low-value fixes because they’re easy, not strategic.
    2. Over-prioritising reactive tasks at the expense of larger, longer-term opportunities.
    3. Creating a cycle where the team is constantly pulled to “just fix this” without systemic improvement.
- FIX real-world positives:
    1. **Amazon’s one-click patent licensing** 
    Immediate, low-risk change that unlocked significant licensing revenue.
    2. **Apple’s rapid software patch** 
    For FaceTime group call bug (2019) - quickly restored user trust and avoided PR escalation.
    3. **Southwest Airlines’ baggage system fix** 
    Ahead of holiday travel — removed a predictable choke point to protect peak revenue.
- FIX real-world negatives:
    1. **Walmart’s same-day delivery fee tweak (2022)** 
    Simple pricing change that produced a short-term lift but didn’t solve the larger logistical bottlenecks impacting customer experience.
    2. **Pfizer’s website booking optimisation during vaccine rollout (2021)** 
    Predictable and urgent improvement to appointment flows, but didn’t address the broader supply/demand imbalance, so frustration persisted.
    3. **OpenAI restoring GPT-4o to the GPT-5 model switcher (2025)**
    Fast, predictable change that appeased vocal user dissatisfaction, but underscored precarious dependence on paying subscribers and a reactive approach to product decisions rather than a clear, consistent long-term strategy.

- **FIX Upside**:
    - Immediate, tangible results with minimal uncertainty.
    - Protects or boosts value without requiring major resource commitments.
    - Builds trust with stakeholders by delivering quick wins.
- **FIX Risk/Downside**:
    - Can create a reactive culture where easy wins crowd out strategic work.
    - Repeated fixes without systemic changes lead to recurring issues.
    - Short term wins often mask long term trade-offs.

#### FIX Failure modes
- **Reactive Drift**: Team defaults to urgent low-hanging fruit instead of strategic initiatives.
- **Whack-a-Mole Cycle**: Fixing symptoms without addressing root causes.
- **Value Inflation**: Overestimating value of the fix because it’s immediate and sure to work.
- **Urgency Creep**: Every minor issue is reframed as a high-value urgent fix.
- **Blind Spot Maintenance**: Fixing what’s visible and obvious, ignoring hidden systemic problems.

#### FIX Error handling
- **Root Cause Check**: Confirm the fix addresses the actual source of the issue.
- **Value Validation**: Quantify the true impact before committing resources.
- **Strategic Balance**: Limit total time allocated to fixes to preserve focus on long-term work.
- **Batching**: Group multiple small fixes together to minimise disruption.
- **Follow-through Audit**: Track whether fixes stay fixed or reappear.

### 8. Urgent / Certainty / Effort: CRUNCH
Time-sensitive, high-effort work with a predictable outcome.  
Often necessary to protect value, meet commitments, or avoid penalties, but offers little upside beyond fulfilling the requirement.

- Types of CRUNCH are:
    1. **Compliance-based:** Urgent regulatory or contractual work with fixed deadlines and non-negotiable scope.
    2. **Operational-based:** High-effort process or fulfilment tasks that must be done immediately to sustain operations.
    3. **Recovery-based:** Urgent restoration of systems or services where the resolution process is known but labor-intensive.
    4. **Delivery-based:** Large, resource-heavy deliverables with immovable short-term deadlines.

#### CRUNCH examples
- CRUNCH positives:
    1. Completing a last-minute compliance report to meet a regulatory deadline.
    2. Producing a customer order that requires all-hands overtime to deliver on time.
    3. Restoring service after a data centre outage using known protocols.
- CRUNCH negatives:
    1. Allowing repeated last-minute crunches to become normal rather than the exception.
    2. Devoting large amounts of team energy to predictable but low-strategic-value deliverables.
    3. Neglecting long-term improvements that would reduce the frequency of CRUNCHs.
- CRUNCH real-world positives:
    1. **Tesla’s end-of-quarter delivery pushes** 
    Predictable but high-effort logistics sprints to meet sales targets.
    2. **IRS annual tax season processing surge** 
    Intense, time-bound workload with a known outcome.
    3. **Apple’s annual iPhone launch supply chain ramp-up** 
    Huge, predictable operational lift to meet launch demand.
- CRUNCH real-world negatives:
    1. **Ever Given Suez Canal clearance operation (2021)** 
    Urgent, predictable resolution steps requiring massive coordinated effort, but no long-term strategic benefit.
    2. **United Airlines holiday scheduling crunch (2022)** 
    High-effort, predictable work to staff flights, caused by avoidable planning gaps.
    3. **Equifax compliance system overhaul after 2017 breach settlement** 
    Urgent, resource-heavy work mandated by legal agreement, with a clear and predictable scope, but delivering no strategic upside beyond compliance.

- **CRUNCH Upside**:
    - Predictable path to resolution builds confidence in delivery.
    - Can protect critical value or avoid significant losses.
    - Demonstrates operational resilience under pressure.
- **CRUNCH Risk/Downside**:
    - Consumes resources without building new strategic advantage.
    - Can mask systemic inefficiencies if not addressed after completion.
    - Creates burnout risk when frequent or prolonged.

#### CRUNCH Failure modes
- **Perpetual Crunch**: Urgent high-effort work becomes a recurring pattern.
- **Value Blindness**: Effort is justified purely by urgency, not strategic worth.
- **Systemic Neglect**: Root causes of repeated CRUNCHs are never addressed.
- **Operational Drag**: Sustained CRUNCH periods slow or derail strategic initiatives.
- **Morale Erosion**: Team fatigue and frustration build when CRUNCH work dominates.

#### CRUNCH Error handling
- **Post-Mortem Analysis**: After each CRUNCH, identify and address the root causes.
- **Frequency Limits**: Set thresholds for how often CRUNCHs are acceptable.
- **Effort Containment**: Define strict boundaries for resource use during a CRUNCH.
- **Parallel Prevention Work**: Run improvement projects alongside CRUNCH work to reduce recurrence.
- **Rotation & Recovery**: Rotate staff and build in recovery time after intense periods.

## TMM in Action: The 2D Linear Roadmap

Each quadrant represents a distinct mode of action:

| Quadrant                      | Definition                                        | Action Focus                                                               |
| ----------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| MOONSHOT (Important + Impact) | High-leverage, paradigm-shifting initiatives.     | Big, bold, strategic moves with uncertain outcomes.                        |
| EXPERIMENT (Important + Certainty)  | Clear, structured tests with clear criteria. | Structure the vision into meaningful clarifying EXPERIMENTs to verify the MOONSHOT.  |
| AIMS (Urgent + Impact)     | Measurable milestones that drive real momentum.   | Set specific deadlines and high-impact checkpoints to focus execution.     |
| PROBLEMS (Urgent + Certainty) | Obstructions preventing execution and progress.   | Systematically remove barriers using Delete / Delay / Delegate / Diminish. |

### Moving Between Quadrants
The key to using TMM effectively is fluidity - moving ideas from one quadrant to another in a structured progression rather than getting stuck in any single one.

| Progression Path    | Process                                                   |
| ------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------- |
| MOONSHOT > EXPERIMENT     | Convert vision into structured, meaningful test.       |
| EXPERIMENT > AIMS      | Define clear aims for test that drive momentum.              |
| AIMS > PROBLEMS  | Identify and eliminate bottlenecks slowing execution.     |
| PROBLEMS > MOONSHOT | Use problem-solving insights to refine future Mooshots. |

Breakthrough Insight: The power of TMM is in placing high-leverage vision (Importance + Impact) at the same psychological level as short-term execution.

## Example: Using 2D Linear Roadmap for Planning

### 1. Identify Your Current Quadrant
Before taking action, determine where your current challenge or opportunity sits within TMM:
- MOONSHOT?: Are you working on something high-leverage but uncertain?
- EXPERIMENT? Are you defining the vision with a real-world test?
- AIMS? Are you setting clear targets as part of your EXPERIMENT?
- PROBLEMS? Are you stuck on an issue preventing the EXPERIMENT from happening?

### 2. Take the Next Logical Step
Once identified, move to the next logical quadrant:
- If in MOONSHOT, structure it into a meaningful EXPERIMENT.
- If in EXPERIMENT, set clear AIMS (targets with deadlines and criteria).
- If in AIMS, sytematically identify, articulate and remove PROBLEMS.
- If in PROBLEMS:
    - For unclear or uncertain problems apply "Strategic Problem Solver" (Where? What? Why? Ways? Which? When?) 
    - For clear, certain problems apply 4Ds (Delete / Delay / Delegate / Diminish) to resolve issues. 

### 3. Ensure Execution is Balanced
Avoid stagnation by ensuring an iterative flow between strategic vision and tactical execution.
- If stuck in MOONSHOT mode -> design a measurable EXPERIMENT.
- If stuck in EXPERIMENT -> define the AIMS.
- If overwhelmed with AIMS -> eliminate PROBLEMS.
- If stuck in PROBLEMS -> focus on MOONSHOT.
