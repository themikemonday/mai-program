# Genius 01 — Driver

Week 1 of Stated Genius vs Revealed Genius. Driver is the *why* — what motivates this person — across three orientations: Becoming (growth, expansion, future possibility), Knowing (truth, certainty, "what is real?"), and Doing (action, solving, present-moment engagement).

This file has two halves:
- **Stated** — Mike's actual questions from the live call, for MAIDRIVER processing
- **Revealed** — observation instructions for MAIEND step 4b

## Stated: The Live Call Questions

These are the actual questions Mike asked on the live call (2026-04-06), not the prepared question set that ships with MAIDRIVER. Use them to inform analysis of the participant's voice transcript stored in `MAI-MIND/MAI-PERCEPTION/GENIUSLOG/raw-genius-log-01.md`. The participant's voice answers are responses to these specific prompts — match each section of the answer to the question that produced it where possible.

### Pre-question framing

Mike said this to participants before asking the questions:

> When I ask you a question and your answer is "well, do you mean this or do you mean that?", my universal answer is yes. The questions are intentionally sometimes Yoda-like or inscrutable. So whatever you mean — because even the way that you answer the questions is signal in itself, and your interpretation of the questions is signal in itself. If I start telling you what my interpretation of the questions is, then I'm giving you my signal. And I'm not you. Lucky you.

The participant's interpretation of an ambiguous question is itself data. Confusion, hesitation, or "I can't hold the question in my head" are signal — they reveal what the participant's driver does and doesn't naturally process. Do not flatten ambiguity.

### Question 1 — Why do you want to improve?

**Initial ask:**
> What motivates you to get better? Why do you want to improve? When you're thinking about improving, when you think about the just improving area, when you're trying to get better, are you more focused on the external result? So you improve because you want a better external result, or are you more focused on your own growth? Is the improvement itself the thing that motivates you, or is it what you are doing when you are improving? Is that what motivates you? I mean, maybe all of them do, but which is the one that you kind of feel more drawn to and why?

**Re-asked (when participants couldn't hold it):**
> Are you more focused on the external result? When you think about improvement, are you more interested in making a better thing or better things? Or are you more interested in actually your own growth, you becoming better? Or are you actually focused on the process of improving? Which is the one that really kind of drives you? And really importantly, why is that the case? It may well be all of them. I get that. But why is that the case?

**Deepening riff:**
> Take a look at what you've written or said. In what situations is this not the case? In what situations is this different? Speak about what those situations are and how it's different. So there might be certain situations in which you are really, really focused. Like, say you say "well, actually, I really enjoy just doing the thing that means I improve, except in this situation, I'm actually focused on something else." So what are those situations and what are you focused on there? Maybe in certain situations, you're mega focused on the result. In other situations, you're actually focused on your future, your future self, your growth. In other situations, you just really love getting into it and just doing the thing. Where is this different? Where doesn't this hold? And why?

### Question 2 — What do you like about solving problems?

> With the kind of problems that you like to solve — and remember, when I use the word problems, I'm talking about good things, puzzly type. The type of problems that you enjoy solving — what is it that you like about solving them? Do you most love getting better at solving them? Maybe it's finding the solution, or having the solution. What is important to you about the kind of problems that you solve? What is motivating you to solve them? Why those specific problems and why do you want to solve them? Is it something to do with you getting better, finding the truth, having the solution, or just the act of actually solving them itself? There's something about doing it. Why is that important to you?

### Question 3 — What makes you feel most alive?

**Initial ask:**
> When you're working on something, what makes you feel most alive? In fact, maybe even try and put into an order. You've got three options. Number one, expanding yourself. Number two, knowing the truth of the matter. Or number three, taking action. Which is the one that makes you feel most alive — expanding yourself, knowing the truth, or taking action? And crucially, what is it about that that you love that makes you feel most alive?

**Deepening riff:**
> Take a look at the last couple of things that you've written or said. Under what conditions does this change? So you might have written a load of stuff about "oh, I am drawn to expanding myself, except when I'm on deadline and then I just want to take action." Or it might be the opposite way around. There might be certain situations — you might be in a certain role. When I am being a parent, then actually what I'm focused on is this. Or when I am doing my taxes, then it all becomes about knowing — actually not knowing the truth, trying to ignore the terrible. What are the specific situations in which what you've just said changes — and what does it change to? What becomes the most important and why?

### Question 4 — Hitting a roadblock in something you love

> Let's say you're working on something that you actually find very interesting. You're really, really into it. But you hit a roadblock. You hit a challenge. It gets really, really tricky. What do you tend to find yourself doing in that situation? Do you tend to create new ideas — is that what you kind of just automatically start, just going to splurge out a bunch of ideas? Do you tend to try and find out facts, what maybe what other people have done? Or do you tend to focus on how you can fix it — like by actually not finding out from elsewhere what's going on, but actually just going into the problem? "Okay, so what's going on here?" This is when you're working on something that you're very interested in, but you've hit a roadblock of some kind. Why do you think you are pulled into that mode of being — either creating ideas, finding out what has already worked maybe from someone else or somewhere else, or by actually going into the problem and trying to solve it? Those are three different ways of approaching a roadblock. What tends to be your default? And if by any chance it's different in different situations, then speak that. Speak your truth.

### Question 5 — When you're worried about something

> Let's say you're worried about something, something's really bothering you. When you're in that kind of situation, where does your mind tend to focus? Does it tend to focus on what could happen in the future? Does it tend to get drawn to what has happened in the past — tend to compare the situation now with what's happened in the past? Do you tend to focus on what is happening, like the actual thing that's happening?
>
> So first of all, think about it in three different areas and make this clear to the AI when you speak. The first area is an area that you love. You're really, really love to work in it, but you're worried about something in it. Do you tend to focus on what could happen? On what has happened in the past? Or on what is happening?
>
> Then think about an area that you actually don't like. You find really grindy and annoying, you don't want to do it, and you're worried about it. In that situation, do you tend to focus on what could happen in the future, on what has happened in the past, or on what is happening now?
>
> So we've got two different situations — one in an area of work or something that you love to do, and another area that you tend to avoid and don't like doing. A way of doing this is to actually think of a specific example you can think of. It's a way of really getting clear on it.

### Question 6 — Uncertainty and doubt

> Let's talk about uncertainty. Doubt. When you're not sure what to do, what do you tend to do to feel more certain, maybe to calm yourself down? What do you naturally do? Do you tend to look to the future when the thing has already been done? Do you tend to look at what other people have done in a similar situation? Or does your mind naturally get pulled into what you can do right now? So when you are feeling uncertain and doubtful, do you tend to look to the future and think "well, okay, it's all right, because in the future I would have done it and it'll be fine and I'll know"? Or do you tend to go "hang on, what have they done? How have they done it?" Or "okay, what do I have in front of me right now?" Which do you tend to focus on? How do you deal with uncertainty naturally? And why is that? What is it about that kind of pull that pulls you?

## Revealed: Session-End Observation

If `USER-CODEC/` exists in this participant's repo, perform a genius observation at the end of each session.

This is the "Revealed" half of Stated Genius vs Revealed Genius. Your job is to capture what you actually saw the participant do this session. The gap between what they said in the live exercise and what they did during the week is the coaching data.

### What drives this person?

Are they motivated by growth and possibility (**Becoming**), truth and certainty (**Knowing**), or action and solving (**Doing**)?

### Three lenses

After the ATS capture (step 4 of MAIEND), reflect on the session through three lenses. These are the same three lenses MAIDRIVER uses for the Stated side — observation here, self-report there. Each lens is optional. Only log what you genuinely observed. Never invent a signal that wasn't there.

**Wanting** — When this person had free choice in the session and no one was pushing, what did they default to? What did they reach for unprompted? What seemed to motivate them when nothing external was pulling?

**Acting** — When the situation got harder, when stakes shifted, when something pressured them — what did they actually do? How did their behaviour change at the beginning vs middle vs end of something?

**Knowing** — Across the floors, areas, and aims they touched this session, did their driver shift between domains? Did they show up differently in one area vs another, or in one role vs another?

### Format

Append to `USER-CODEC/genius-observations.md` (create the file if it does not exist):

```
### YYYY-MM-DD
**Wanting:** [1-2 sentences. What they defaulted to with free choice. Quote behaviour, not interpretation. Note which Driver dimension it might signal — Becoming/Knowing/Doing — and why.]
**Acting:** [1-2 sentences. How they behaved under pressure or shifting conditions. Quote behaviour. Driver dimension + why.]
**Knowing:** [1-2 sentences. How their driver shifted across areas or roles. Quote behaviour. Driver dimension + why.]
```

Skip any lens you don't have a genuine observation for. If nothing relevant was observed this session, skip silently — do not invent an entry to fill the slots.
